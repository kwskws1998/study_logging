Research notes

PPO Model Architecture

2026.06.29 10 min

PPO is a policy-based deep reinforcement learning algorithm that balances performance and sample efficiency while stabilizing policy updates. The paper's core move is to reuse sampled data for several optimization epochs, but to stop the objective from rewarding policy changes that are too large.

Paper anchor: clipped probability ratios, multiple epochs of minibatch updates, lower bound, entropy bonus.

The Starting Point: Policy Gradient

The basic policy-gradient estimator uses the log probability of the action taken at timestep \(t\), multiplied by an advantage estimate. If the advantage is positive, the update increases the probability of that action. If the advantage is negative, the update decreases it.

\[ \hat{g} = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\hat{A}_t \right] \]
  • \(\log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\): log probability of taking action \(a_t\) in state \(s_t\).
  • \(\hat{A}_t\): advantage estimate, meaning how much better the sampled action looked relative to the baseline for that state.
  • The optimization target is maximized in the RL formulation. In code that minimizes losses, this objective is usually multiplied by \(-1\).

The Problem: Step Size and Sample Efficiency

Vanilla policy gradient can be unstable if the network takes too many large updates on one batch of data. This is especially dangerous in RL because the model creates its own future data through its policy. It also tends to be sample-inefficient when data is used once and then discarded.

Trust Region: Addressing Instability

PPO wants the new policy \(\pi_{\theta}\) to remain close to the old policy \(\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}\), while still moving enough to improve. Instead of enforcing the full TRPO-style constraint, PPO uses a clipped surrogate objective to create a practical trust-region-like effect.

PPO introduces the probability ratio \(r_t(\theta)\), which measures how much the probability of the sampled action changed under the new policy.

\[ r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)} {\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a_t \mid s_t)} \]
  • \(r_t = 1\): the sampled action is equally likely under the old and new policies.
  • \(r_t > 1\): the sampled action is more likely under the new policy.
  • \(0 < r_t < 1\): the sampled action is less likely under the new policy.

The Clipped Surrogate Objective

The unclipped objective gives a raw incentive:

\[ r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t \]

If the advantage is high, this term can become very large as the policy ratio moves away from \(1\). PPO therefore clips the ratio, usually with \(\epsilon = 0.2\), so the effective ratio is bounded between \(0.8\) and \(1.2\).

\[ L^{\mathrm{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\, \operatorname{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right) \right] \]

Taking the minimum of the unclipped and clipped terms makes the objective pessimistic: it uses the lower of the two values, so large policy-ratio changes stop providing extra useful incentive.

Why the Clipped Objective Works

When \(\hat{A}_t > 0\), the sampled action was better than expected. The unclipped term wants to increase its probability. The clipped term caps the useful increase at \(1+\epsilon\), so a very large probability increase does not keep improving the objective.

When \(\hat{A}_t < 0\), the sampled action was worse than expected. The update should reduce its probability. The clipped term prevents the objective from rewarding an excessive drop below \(1-\epsilon\). Once the term is capped, the gradient contribution for that datapoint can become zero.

Advantage Estimation: Critic

PPO is commonly implemented as an actor-critic method. The actor \(\pi_\theta\) decides which action to take. The critic \(V_\phi\) predicts the value of the state. The advantage \(\hat{A}_t\) tells the actor whether the sampled action was better or worse than the critic's baseline expectation.

The slide intuition "actual reward minus predicted reward" is close in spirit, but for PPO's truncated GAE the actual computation is based on TD errors accumulated over a fixed horizon.

Generalized Advantage Estimation

To compute the advantage used for the policy update, PPO can use truncated GAE. It first computes the one-step TD error:

\[ \delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t) \]

Then it sums future TD errors with discounting by \(\gamma\) and \(\lambda\):

\[ \hat{A}_t = \delta_t + (\gamma\lambda)\delta_{t+1} + \cdots + (\gamma\lambda)^{T-t+1}\delta_{T-1} \]
  • \(\lambda = 1\): relies more on the sampled trajectory, so it can be noisy.
  • \(\lambda = 0\): uses the one-step TD error, leaning more on the critic.
  • The PPO paper reports \(\lambda = 0.95\) in its benchmark hyperparameter tables.

The Objective Maximized by PPO

PPO combines the clipped policy surrogate, a value-function error term, and an entropy term for exploration:

\[ L_t^{\mathrm{CLIP+VF+S}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ L_t^{\mathrm{CLIP}}(\theta) - c_1 L_t^{\mathrm{VF}}(\theta) + c_2 S[\pi_\theta](s_t) \right] \]
\[ L_t^{\mathrm{VF}} = \left(V_\theta(s_t) - V_t^{\mathrm{target}}\right)^2 \]
  • \(L_t^{\mathrm{CLIP}}\): actor-side clipped surrogate objective.
  • \(L_t^{\mathrm{VF}}\): critic-side squared value error.
  • \(S[\pi_\theta](s_t)\): entropy bonus for sufficient exploration.
  • \(c_1, c_2\): coefficients controlling the value loss and entropy bonus.

The Algorithm of PPO

Each iteration, \(N\) actors collect data by taking actions for \(T\) timesteps under the old policy. PPO computes advantage estimates, constructs the clipped surrogate loss on the collected \(NT\) timesteps, and then optimizes that same dataset for \(K\) epochs using minibatch SGD or Adam.

  1. Run \(\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}\) in the environment for \(T\) timesteps.
  2. Collect \((s_t, a_t, r_t)\) and compute \(\hat{A}_1, \ldots, \hat{A}_T\).
  3. Optimize the surrogate objective for \(K\) epochs with minibatches.
  4. Update \(\theta_{\mathrm{old}} \leftarrow \theta\), discard the old batch, and repeat.

Takeaway

PPO keeps the intuitive direction of policy gradient: increase good actions and decrease bad actions. The clipped probability ratio is what makes repeated minibatch optimization safer, because the objective stops rewarding updates once the new policy moves too far from the old policy on the sampled action.

Source: Schulman et al., Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms.
